The Suburban Swing

How the space between city and countryside became the most consequential — and restless — zone in Western democracy.

By Evgeniya Orlova  ·  April 2025  ·  US · NL · FR · IT
01 — Introduction

The geography of discontent

In election after election across the Western world, the same pattern has emerged: cities lean left, rural areas lean right, and the suburbs — that vast, sprawling middle ground — go whichever way the political wind is blowing. But to call suburbs merely "swing" territory understates their significance. They are, increasingly, the engine of democratic change.

This report examines suburban voting patterns across four major Western democracies — the United States (presidential election, 2024), the Netherlands (parliamentary elections, October 29 2025), France (legislative elections 2024 and presidential election 2022), and Italy (parliamentary elections 2022). Drawing on electoral geography data, it tests two hypotheses about why suburbs swing.

Hypothesis 1

Suburbs are structurally volatile

Unlike cities (stable left) or rural areas (stable right), suburbs contain the socio-economic mix that makes them responsive to immediate material concerns over ideological loyalty.

✓ Confirmed across all cases
Hypothesis 2

Right-wing parties capture suburban anxieties more effectively

Right-wing populist and conservative parties have outperformed in suburbs by addressing cost of living, safety, housing, and immigration — quintessentially suburban concerns.

~ Largely confirmed, with nuance

The suburban reversal

The 2024 US election was decided in the suburbs. In 2020, the "suburban revolt" against Trump delivered Biden's victory. By 2024, many of those same suburbs shifted back toward Republicans, driven primarily by inflation, housing costs, and cost of living.

US Key Findings
  • Suburban areas delivered decisive margins in six of seven key swing states: PA, MI, WI, AZ, GA, NV.
  • The sharpest rightward shift was in the outer suburban ring — most exposed to inflation in housing, food, and transport.
  • High-income, college-educated inner suburbs (Loudoun VA, Chester PA) remained competitive for Democrats.
  • Latino suburban communities in AZ, NV, TX moved toward Republicans by 8–14 points, reflecting the same material-anxiety pattern.

Netherlands 2025 Key Findings
  • Amsterdam voted GL-PvdA + D66 combined 50.8% — PVV placed fifth with 7.3%, one of its lowest scores anywhere. The "Amsterdam = PVV" framing is factually wrong.
  • Rotterdam and The Hague flipped: PVV was largest in both cities in 2023, but fell to 3rd in 2025 as D66 surged from ~7–8% to ~17–20%.
  • Almere and Capelle illustrate the persistent suburban PVV premium: PVV leads, but its margins have roughly halved since 2023 (from 36% to 19% in Almere; 27% to 18% in Capelle).
  • Utrecht is the most left-leaning large city: PVV at 6.8%, with D66 and GL-PvdA nearly tied at ~26% each.
  • The combined far-right (PVV + JA21 + FvD = 42 seats) still grew vs 2023's 40, even as PVV individually fell — suburban anxiety redistributed within the right bloc.

France's city-suburb divide is the sharpest in this dataset. Paris, Marseille, Lyon, and Toulouse voted predominantly NFP in 2024; RN won just seven seats in urban areas nationally, all in the south-east. France Key Findings — 2022 & 2024
  • Paris, Lyon, Marseille, Toulouse, Bordeaux all voted predominantly NFP in the 2024 legislative election. RN won only 7 seats in urban areas nationally — all in the south-east (3 in Nice, 3 in Marseille, 1 in Montpellier).
  • The périurbain–city gap is the sharpest in the dataset: IDF outer ring vs Paris core = +29pp in 2022, +24.7pp in 2024 — consistent across both elections.
  • Le Pen won outright majority in PACA (54.2%) and Hauts-de-France (53.4%) in 2022 — both dominated by périurbain and ex-industrial suburban voters.
  • The figures between the two maps are not directly comparable (2nd round vs 1st round format), but the geographic ranking is identical — same zones lead in both years.
  • Macron's coalition lost the périurbain entirely — it now concentrates in city centres and upper-income inner suburbs only.

Italian cities largely held for the centre-left and PD in 2022, while FdI's gains were concentrated in suburban commuter belts. In southern cities, M5S rather than the right captured the urban discontented vote.

Italy Key Findings
  • PD led FdI in city cores nationally (22.1% vs 20.8%) — the right's urban underperformance mirrors the pattern in France and the Netherlands.
  • FdI's suburban premium averaged +9pp over city cores: Bologna hinterland 29.7% vs city 19.5%; Milano suburbs 28.9% vs city 18.7%.
  • Lazio (Roma hinterland) is FdI's home turf — the Castelli Romani and Pomezia belt where the party has deep local roots. Roma city itself was more contested.
  • In southern cities (Naples, Palermo), M5S remained dominant, limiting FdI and preventing a simple right-suburb equation in the south.
  • FdI drew broadly: ~70% of its 2022 voters came from other centre-right parties (Lega, FI), with ~17% from former M5S and ~11% from PD — cross-cutting suburban appeal.

The suburban voter is not voting against democracy. They are voting against the price of a litre of milk, a mortgage renewal, and a 45-minute commute on a road that hasn't been resurfaced since 2009.

The Dutch exception — what it tells us

The 2025 Netherlands election introduces a crucial nuance. D66's surge showed that suburban voters can be pulled back from the far right when a credible alternative contests their material concerns — above all, housing. But D66 did not win the suburbs; it won the cities. Almere, Capelle, and Maastricht all stayed with PVV even as Rotterdam and The Hague flipped. The far-right total (PVV + JA21 + FvD = 42 seats) actually grew. This is the key analytical finding: suburban swing operates primarily within the right bloc, not between left and right.

04 — Conclusions

Who owns the suburb?

For now, the right — but cities remain reliably left or centre-left across all four cases. Amsterdam voted GL-PvdA + D66 at over 50%. Paris gave RN 14%, with NFP winning 12 of 18 seats. Bologna and Turin's city centres stayed with PD even as their hinterlands moved right. This urban-suburban polarity is the defining electoral geography of the 2020s.

The suburb's defining characteristic is volatility, conditional on material outcomes. The Netherlands 2025 case is the strongest proof: the same suburban electorate that gave PVV a landslide in 2023 punished it in 2025 — while still not moving left. Whether centre-left parties can reclaim the suburb depends on whether they can rebuild a vocabulary of material concern that speaks to the périurbain, the Randstad commuter belt, and the Roman hinterland. So far, they have not.

H1 — Final Verdict

Suburbs are structurally volatile

Confirmed across all four cases. NL 2025 is the strongest proof: PVV's suburban base punished it severely in one cycle while cities remained stable for left and centre parties.
