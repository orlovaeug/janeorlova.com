§ 1
Overview & Key Findings

First Impressions Shape
Political Judgements

This survey-experimental study examined the degree to which physical appearance influences political perception across five dimensions: physical attractiveness, trustworthiness, competence, approachability, and perceived leadership potential. Respondents rated three Dutch political figures presented as Persons A, B, and C — corresponding to Rob Jetten, Thierry Baudet, and Dick Schoof — without explicit identification cues in the instrument design.

The results align with a substantial body of political psychology literature suggesting that superficial appearance cues — processed rapidly and often unconsciously — exert measurable influence on voters' assessments of political suitability. Our findings document a consistent perceptual hierarchy across all five measured dimensions, with meaningful variation by political engagement level, European cultural identity, and gender.

Principal Finding
Across all measured dimensions, perceived competence is the strongest single predictor of leadership suitability (Jetten r = .791; Baudet r = .644; Schoof r = .710). Physical appearance, while significant, operates primarily as an indirect cue — filtering initial impressions that are subsequently refined via trustworthiness and approachability assessments. Appearance is where perception begins; competence is where it lands.

§ 3
Profession Attribution

What Profession Do
They Look Like?

Beyond numerical ratings, respondents answered an open-text question: "What profession do you think this person most likely has?" These free associations provide a window into the implicit mental models respondents apply when decoding political faces — and carry significant implications for political communication strategy, visual branding, and voter perception research. Responses were coded into seven profession categories by independent reviewers.

The distribution reveals meaningfully different appearance archetypes for each politician. Critically, the diversity of guesses is itself a finding: a wide spread across categories indicates an appearance that resists coherent archetype formation, which generally suppresses leadership perception among low-information audiences.

Profession Attribution — Strategic Insight
Jetten's appearance activates a dual signal — political authority (30%) combined with corporate and technical competence (Business Executive 21%, Finance 15%, Tech 15%). This is the most coherent archetype in the study: his look reads as institutionally capable regardless of whether the viewer knows his political role. The technology and engineering associations are particularly notable for a climate-focused political brand — they suggest modernity and forward-orientation without explicit reference to politics.

Baudet's profile is the most elite-but-ambiguous: Finance and Legal together account for 39% of attributions, alongside notable Actor and Architecture mentions. His appearance communicates high-status formation without warmth or institutional accountability. This maps directly onto his low approachability score (M = 4.53) and the competence–trust split that defines his perceptual profile. Respondents recognise something accomplished; they do not recognise something trustworthy.

Schoof generates the most heterogeneous free-associations — including "Petty Theft," "Car salesman," and "Retired physician" alongside legitimate political attributions. While he has the highest politician recognition rate (34%), this recognition did not translate into leadership preference. The academic and medical associations (9%) hint at a bureaucratic visual register consistent with his intelligence service background — professionally credible, but not political in the aspirational sense voters tend to reward.
"Jetten reads as executive; Baudet as elite-ambiguous; Schoof as bureaucratic. Each archetype carries its own ceiling for public leadership perception."

§ 5
Predictors of Leadership Perception

What Drives the
Leadership Impression?

Pearson correlation coefficients were computed between each perceptual dimension and the leadership rating for each politician. The results reveal a consistent pattern: competence is the dominant predictor of perceived leadership suitability, followed by approachability and trustworthiness. Physical appearance, while correlated, operates as a more distal cue — influencing first impressions but not directly determining leadership judgements.

Communication Implication
For political communication professionals, the data suggest that projecting competence through visual and rhetorical cues should be the primary objective in image strategy — not physical attractiveness per se. Approachability shows consistent mid-range correlations (r = .508–.554) across all three politicians, indicating it acts as a moderating variable that converts perceived competence into actual leadership trust. The pooled cross-politician appearance–leadership correlation of r = .517 confirms that physical presentation is strategically relevant but insufficient in isolation.

European respondents assign notably higher leadership ratings to Jetten (M = 7.06 vs M = 5.84 among non-European respondents) — a 1.22-point gap that likely reflects prior political knowledge and positive opinion rather than pure appearance effects. Baudet's ratings are comparatively stable across this divide (5.61 vs 5.36). Trust vote by European identity further compounds the pattern: European respondents favour Jetten at 77.8% vs 68.0% among non-European respondents, while Baudet receives only 5.6% from European respondents versus 24.0% from non-European — a striking disparity almost certainly driven by recognition.

Jetten's ratings are virtually gender-neutral (6.38 vs 6.31), indicating broad cross-demographic visual appeal — a strategically valuable attribute.

§ 8
The Baudet Paradox

High Competence, Low Trust:
A Perceptual Split

Baudet presents a theoretically important perceptual profile. His competence rating (M = 6.12, SD = 2.06) substantially exceeds his trust score (M = 5.07, SD = 2.07) — a 1.05-point gap that is the largest competence–trustworthiness divergence in the study. For Jetten, the equivalent gap is only 0.32 points (6.65 vs 6.33); for Schoof, 0.54 points. The profession data amplifies this: Baudet is the only politician who attracted "Actor" as a free-text response — a category that blends presence and performance-of-credibility without connoting genuine institutional authority.

This pattern is consistent with what political psychologists term a competence–integrity trade-off: a figure perceived as capable but not trustworthy or value-aligned. His approachability deficit (M = 4.53 — the lowest of any politician on any dimension in the study) reinforces this reading. He signals high ability but low warmth and relational accessibility — a combination that suppresses leadership perception even when competence cues are relatively strong.

§ 9
Limitations & Confounds

Methodological Considerations

Recognition bias. A significant proportion of European respondents correctly identified one or more politicians within the open-text profession field — referencing "Dick Schoof, former prime minister," "Rob Jetten, our new prime minister," and "FvD founder, conspiracy propagandist." This contamination cannot be disentangled from appearance-based impressions. Future iterations should use unfamiliar politicians, digitally altered stimuli, or include a post-hoc recognition check with exclusion protocols.

Sample composition. The sample skews young (predominantly 18–34) and female, which limits generalisability to the broader Dutch electorate. The European/non-European analytical split is useful but subgroup sizes are insufficient for robust multivariate modelling or mediation analysis.

Response quality. One respondent assigned perfect scores of 10 to Schoof across all dimensions — a clear outlier likely reflecting confusion or a non-serious response, marginally inflating Schoof's averages. A stricter attention-check protocol is recommended for future waves.

Common method variance. All five perceptual dimensions were collected from the same respondent in the same session, meaning dimension–leadership correlations are likely inflated relative to a multi-source or longitudinal design. The strong competence–leadership correlations (r = .644–.791) should be interpreted with this caveat.

§ 10
Conclusions

Conclusions for Political
Communication Practice

This study confirms that physical appearance significantly shapes political perception — but operates through a structured hierarchy of inferential cues rather than directly. Appearance is an entry point, not a destination: it primes assessments of competence, trustworthiness, and approachability, which collectively determine whether a leader impression crystallises into genuine preference.

The profession attribution data add a qualitative layer that considerably enriches the quantitative findings. The fact that Jetten's appearance triggers associations with executive leadership and technology — rather than solely politics — may account for part of his cross-partisan appeal: he reads as institutionally authoritative to audiences who do not yet know his political position. Baudet's more ambiguous archetype (elite professional, actor, financial figure) generates competence without warmth — a perceptual trap that approachability-focused communication work could theoretically address. Schoof's highly dispersed attributions suggest a visual identity that has not yet coalesced into a coherent archetype, limiting his capacity to generate strong spontaneous leadership impressions.

Strategic Recommendation
Political image management should prioritise the triad of competence projection, trustworthiness signalling, and approachability cues — in that order of explanatory power, and in recognition that each element moderates the others. Physical attractiveness enhances overall impression but does not independently drive leadership perception. Any visual communication strategy that optimises appearance at the expense of warm, credible, accessible demeanour is likely to produce Baudet's pattern: high perceived ability, low trust, suppressed leadership preference.
Methodology: Survey-experimental design. Respondents rated three Dutch politicians (A = Rob Jetten, B = Thierry Baudet, C = Dick Schoof) across five perceptual dimensions (1–10 scale). Open-text profession attributions coded into seven categories by independent reviewers; discrepancies resolved by consensus. Paired-sample t-tests used for significance testing of between-politician leadership differences. Pearson r reported for dimension–leadership correlations. Subgroup analyses conducted on European/non-European identity and self-reported gender; political engagement analysed as an ordinal grouping variable. Significance thresholds: * p < .05, *** p < .001 (two-tailed, estimated df ≈ 42). Common-method variance caution applies to all within-instrument correlations. Recognition-contaminated responses retained in the main analysis; sensitivity analyses excluding identified respondents are recommended for future reporting.
