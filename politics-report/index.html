§ 1
Overview & Key Findings

First Impressions Shape
Political Judgements

This survey-experimental study examined the degree to which physical appearance influences political perception across five dimensions: physical attractiveness, trustworthiness, competence, approachability, and perceived leadership potential. Respondents rated three Dutch political figures presented as Persons A, B and C — corresponding respectively to Rob Jetten, Thierry Baudet, and Dick Schoof — without explicit identification cues in the instrument design.

The results align with a substantial body of political psychology literature suggesting that superficial appearance cues — processed rapidly and often unconsciously — exert measurable influence on voters' assessments of political suitability. Our findings document a consistent hierarchy across all five measured dimensions, with meaningful subgroup variation by political engagement, European identity, and gender.

Principal Finding
Across all measured dimensions, perceived competence is the strongest single predictor of leadership suitability (r = .791 for Jetten, r = .644 for Baudet, r = .710 for Schoof). Physical appearance, while significant, operates primarily as an indirect cue — filtering initial impressions that are then refined via trustworthiness and approachability assessments.

Communication Implication
For political strategists, the data suggest that projecting competence through visual and communicative cues should be the primary objective in image management — not physical attractiveness per se. Crucially, approachability shows consistent mid-range correlations (r = .508–.554) across all three politicians, indicating it may act as a moderating variable that converts competence into leadership trust.

The pooled cross-politician correlation between appearance and leadership is r = .517 — a moderate effect that confirms the relevance of physical presentation while underscoring that appearance alone is insufficient. The data are consistent with a mediation model in which appearance shapes competence and trustworthiness impressions, which in turn predict leadership evaluations.

"Competence is perceived — it is communicated through posture, gaze, and grooming before a single word is spoken."
§ 5
Statistical Significance

Between-Politician Differences:
Paired t-Tests

Paired-sample t-tests on leadership ratings confirm that the observed mean differences between politicians are statistically meaningful. Given the within-subject design (each respondent rated all three), paired comparisons control for individual response tendencies and represent conservative significance estimates.

Jetten vs Baudet t = 2.25 Mean diff = 0.88 pts  ·  SDdiff = 2.57  ·  p < .05

Baudet vs Schoof t = 2.16 Mean diff = 0.79 pts  ·  SDdiff = 2.40  ·  p < .05

All three pairwise comparisons reach conventional significance thresholds. The Jetten–Schoof contrast is the strongest (t = 4.02), reflecting a 1.67-point mean leadership gap — the largest in the study. The Jetten–Baudet difference (t = 2.25, Δ = 0.88 pts) is significant but narrower, consistent with the observation that Baudet generates moderate competence impressions that partially offset his lower trustworthiness and approachability scores.

The most politically engaged respondents show the largest gap in favour of Jetten (Δ = 2.43 pts vs Baudet). His image communicates competence most effectively to the already-engaged electorate.
§ 7
The Baudet Paradox

High Competence, Low Trust:
Baudet's Perceptual Split

Baudet presents a theoretically interesting perceptual profile. His competence rating (M = 6.12, SD = 2.06) is notably higher than his trust score (M = 5.07, SD = 2.07) — a 1.05-point gap that is the largest competence–trust divergence in the study. For Jetten, the equivalent gap is only 0.32 points (6.65 vs 6.33); for Schoof, it is 0.54 points.

This pattern is consistent with what political psychologists have termed a competence–integrity trade-off: where a figure is perceived as capable but not honest or aligned with the perceiver's values. Baudet's approachability deficit (M = 4.53, the lowest in the study for any politician on any dimension bar Schoof's approachability) reinforces this reading — he appears to signal high ability but low warmth and credibility.

§ 8
Limitations & Confounds

Methodological Considerations
& Study Limitations

Recognition bias. A significant number of European respondents correctly identified one or more of the three politicians by name within the open-text profession field — referencing "Dick Schoof, former prime minister," "Rob Jetten, our new prime minister," and "FvD founder, conspiracy propagandist" (for Baudet). This recognition introduces prior attitude contamination that cannot be fully separated from appearance-based impressions in the present design. Future iterations should either use unfamiliar politicians, digitally altered faces, or include a post-hoc recognition check with appropriate exclusion criteria.

Sample composition. The sample skews young (predominantly 18–34) and female, which limits generalisability to the broader Dutch electorate. The European/non-European split is analytically useful but the subgroup sizes (EU n=18; non-EU n=25) are insufficient for robust multivariate modelling.

Response quality. One respondent (European, female, "Not at all" engaged) gave a perfect 10 to Schoof across all dimensions and selected her as the most trustworthy leader — a clear outlier that may reflect confusion or a protest response rather than genuine assessment. Her inclusion inflates Schoof's ratings marginally.

Common method variance. Because all five perceptual dimensions were collected from the same respondent in the same instrument, correlations between dimensions (e.g., competence and leadership) are likely inflated relative to what would be found in a multi-source design. The strong competence–leadership correlations should be interpreted with this caveat.

§ 9
Conclusions

Conclusions for Political
Communication Practice

This study confirms that physical appearance significantly shapes political perception — but operates through a structured hierarchy of inferential cues. Appearance functions as an entry point, not a destination: it primes assessments of competence, trustworthiness and approachability, which collectively determine whether a leader impression crystallises.

For political communication professionals, three actionable findings emerge. First, competence signalling is paramount — it is the single strongest predictor of leadership perception across all three politicians and should anchor visual and rhetorical strategy. Second, approachability is the critical moderator: Baudet's failure to convert perceived competence into leadership trust appears structurally linked to his low approachability score, suggesting that warmth and accessibility cues must accompany ability projections. Third, politically engaged audiences are appearance-responsive: contrary to a naive assumption that experts would "see past" appearance, engaged respondents show the largest appearance-linked leadership gaps, suggesting that appearance cues become integrated into — rather than overridden by — political knowledge structures.

Recommendation
Political image management should prioritise the triad of competence projection, trustworthiness signalling, and approachability cues — in that order of explanatory power. Physical attractiveness enhances overall impression but does not independently drive leadership perception. Any visual communication strategy that maximises appearance at the expense of warm, credible demeanour is likely to be sub-optimal.

Methodology note: Survey-experimental design. Respondents rated three Dutch politicians (identified internally as A, B, C) on five perceptual dimensions (1–10 scale). Paired-sample t-tests were used for significance testing of between-politician leadership differences. Pearson r reported for dimension–leadership correlations. Subgroup analyses conducted on European/non-European and gender splits; engagement level analysed as an ordinal grouping variable. Statistical significance thresholds: * p < .05, *** p < .001 (two-tailed, estimated df ≈ 42). Common-method variance caution applies to all within-instrument correlations.
