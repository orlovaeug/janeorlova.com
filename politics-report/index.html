01
Language Diffusion
PVV sets the terms.
The mainstream follows — then loses power.

The dominant pattern across nine years is a consistent fringe-to-mainstream flow of political vocabulary. Terms originating in PVV press releases appear in VVD and CDA communications with a 12–24 month lag. The clearest case: asielstop — first used in PVV manifestos in 2017, reached NSC and VVD by 2023, and entered the Schoof coalition agreement ("Hoop, Lef en Trots," May 2024) as governing policy language.

The reverse flow — from left-progressive parties — operates equally on social and climate topics. Bestaanszekerheid was a PvdA frame from 2020–21, adopted by D66, VVD, and CDA by 2021, and became the defining phrase of that election. Klimaatcrisis (crisis framing, not klimaatverandering) was a Volt/GL frame from 2019 that reached the Jetten coalition agreement in January 2026.

The 2025 elections mark a language regime change. With PVV shrinking from 37 to 26 seats and D66 surging to joint-largest, the vocabulary that dominates plenary debate is shifting. Asylum and migration — the PVV-anchored topics of 2024–25 — are being displaced by European defence, climate investment, and housing.

This creates a natural experiment: will the Jetten cabinet's coalition agreement language — which centres on Europese defensie, woningbouw, and climate framing — now diffuse back from coalition language into opposition party communications? Early 2026 press release analysis suggests PVV has already adopted Europese defensie as an opposition frame — but to oppose, not endorse. The semantic direction of adoption matters as much as its occurrence.

03
Corpus Similarity
Coalition makes parties sound alike.
Divergence predicts collapse.

TF-IDF cosine similarity tracks how similar party press release corpora are over time. The clearest finding: parties governing together adopt more similar vocabulary, and vocabulary divergence appears to precede political divergence. In the months before PVV left the Schoof coalition in June 2025, PVV–VVD cosine similarity on defence/Ukraine terms dropped sharply — while migration-term similarity held. Language divergence preceded the political crisis by approximately 6–8 weeks.

The 2025 elections produced a new similarity landscape. D66 (26 seats) and GL-PvdA (20 seats) — both with high historical vocabulary similarity on EU, climate, and housing — now dominate the progressive-centre space. Their press release corpora have converged further since the Jetten coalition formed.

The most striking distinctiveness finding of 2025: NSC's elimination from the Kamer removed the party with the fastest-converging vocabulary in the corpus. Between 2022 and 2025, NSC's language had moved from a distinctive good-governance/rule-of-law framing toward generic centre-right vocabulary — a linguistic sign of a party that lost its distinctive identity before losing its voters.

BBB shows the same pattern at a slower pace: its highly distinctive agrarian vocabulary of 2022 has been absorbed into generic right-populist framing by 2024–25. After Mona Keijzer left BBB in February 2026, the party's press release vocabulary shifted again — a small but detectable change the corpus captures within weeks.

04
2025 Language Landscape
New government,
new frame competition.

What changed in 2025: NSC's elimination removed the party with the most generic convergent vocabulary. PVV's seat loss reduced the dominance of migration framing in Kamer debate. D66's surge elevated European, climate, and progressive housing vocabulary. CDA's revival brought a distinctive Christian-democratic social framing back into the mainstream.

The Jetten coalition agreement "Aan de slag" (January 2026) introduced three new dominant frames: Europese defensie, woningbouw versnelling, and eigen risico (in healthcare). Of these, the first is genuinely new in coalition language — the second and third are recycled from earlier cycles but now with governing weight.

The new opposition vocabulary competition: With PVV, GL-PvdA, JA21, FvD, and SP all in opposition, the right-wing opposition (PVV+JA21+FvD = 42 seats) and left opposition (GL-PvdA+SP+PvdD = 26 seats) are now developing opposing frames around the Jetten government's language. PVV has already begun framing Europese defensie as capitulating to Brussels — adopting the term to oppose it.

The 2025 corpus will be the most analytically rich in the dataset. A coalition that formed after a right-wing collapse, with a PM who moved his party rightward to win, governing with two parties whose language it partly adopted — the conditions for rapid vocabulary diffusion and counter-framing are all present.

§ 1
Overview & Key Findings

First Impressions Shape
Political Judgements

This survey-experimental study examined the degree to which physical appearance influences political perception across five dimensions: physical attractiveness, trustworthiness, competence, approachability, and perceived leadership potential. Respondents rated three Dutch political figures presented as Persons A, B, and C — corresponding to Rob Jetten, Thierry Baudet, and Dick Schoof — without explicit identification cues in the instrument design.

The results align with a substantial body of political psychology literature suggesting that superficial appearance cues — processed rapidly and often unconsciously — exert measurable influence on voters' assessments of political suitability. Our findings document a consistent perceptual hierarchy across all five measured dimensions, with meaningful variation by political engagement level, European cultural identity, and gender.

Principal Finding
Across all measured dimensions, perceived competence is the strongest single predictor of leadership suitability (Jetten r = .791; Baudet r = .644; Schoof r = .710). Physical appearance, while significant, operates primarily as an indirect cue — filtering initial impressions that are subsequently refined via trustworthiness and approachability assessments. Appearance is where perception begins; competence is where it lands.

§ 3
Profession Attribution

What Profession Do
They Look Like?

Beyond numerical ratings, respondents answered an open-text question: "What profession do you think this person most likely has?" These free associations provide a window into the implicit mental models respondents apply when decoding political faces — and carry significant implications for political communication strategy, visual branding, and voter perception research. Responses were coded into seven profession categories by independent reviewers.

The distribution reveals meaningfully different appearance archetypes for each politician. Critically, the diversity of guesses is itself a finding: a wide spread across categories indicates an appearance that resists coherent archetype formation, which generally suppresses leadership perception among low-information audiences.

Profession Attribution — Strategic Insight
Jetten's appearance activates a dual signal — political authority (30%) combined with corporate and technical competence (Business Executive 21%, Finance 15%, Tech 15%). This is the most coherent archetype in the study: his look reads as institutionally capable regardless of whether the viewer knows his political role.

Baudet's profile is the most elite-but-ambiguous: Finance and Legal together account for 39% of attributions, alongside notable Actor and Architecture mentions. His appearance communicates high-status formation without warmth or institutional accountability.

Schoof generates the most heterogeneous free-associations — including "Petty Theft," "Car salesman," and "Retired physician" alongside legitimate political attributions. While he has the highest politician recognition rate (34%), this recognition did not translate into leadership preference.
"Jetten reads as executive; Baudet as elite-ambiguous; Schoof as bureaucratic. Each archetype carries its own ceiling for public leadership perception."

§ 9
Limitations & Confounds

Methodological Considerations

Recognition bias. A significant proportion of European respondents correctly identified one or more politicians within the open-text profession field. This contamination cannot be disentangled from appearance-based impressions.

Sample composition. The sample skews young (predominantly 18–34) and female, which limits generalisability to the broader Dutch electorate.

Common method variance. All five perceptual dimensions were collected from the same respondent in the same session, meaning dimension–leadership correlations are likely inflated relative to a multi-source or longitudinal design.

§ 10
Conclusions

Conclusions for Political
Communication Practice

This study confirms that physical appearance significantly shapes political perception — but operates through a structured hierarchy of inferential cues. Appearance is an entry point, not a destination: it primes assessments of competence, trustworthiness, and approachability, which collectively determine whether a leader impression crystallises into genuine preference.

Strategic Recommendation
Political image management should prioritise the triad of competence projection, trustworthiness signalling, and approachability cues — in that order of explanatory power. Any visual communication strategy that optimises appearance at the expense of warm, credible, accessible demeanour is likely to produce Baudet's pattern: high perceived ability, low trust, suppressed leadership preference.

§ M
Methodology

Scores zijn berekend door Nederlandstalige tekstcorpora per segment samen te stellen uit echte publieke bronnen (NOS.nl, Tweede Kamer verslagen, VluchtelingenWerk.nl, GroenLinks-PvdA.nl, coalitieakkoord D66/VVD/CDA, rijksoverheid.nl) en te scoren tegen een Nederlands emotielexicon gebaseerd op de NRC Word-Emotion Association Lexicon (Mohammad & Turney, 2013). Emotiecategorieën: vrees [crisis, bedreiging, instroom, onveiligheid, chaos…], woede [falen, onverantwoordelijk, schandalig, bezuinigingen…], hoop [toekomst, integratie, meedoen, samenwerking…], vertrouwen [procedure, wetgeving, akkoord, stabiliteit…]. Tokens worden genormaliseerd naar 100% over de vier categorieën per segment. Steekproeven kleiner dan 30 tokens zijn gemarkeerd.

emotion_map_nl.py haalt live paginatekst op, tokeniseert, verwijdert stopwoorden en berekent trefferfrequenties. The 2025 elections transformed the Tweede Kamer's composition. PVV lost 11 seats (37→26); D66 gained 17 (9→26). NSC, which held 20 seats in 2023, was entirely wiped out. CDA surged from 5 to 18. The result: a Kamer with a markedly different gender geography, because the parties that grew — D66, CDA — had much higher female representation than the parties that dominated in 2023.

65 women were installed on 12 November 2025 — a record. They come from 12 of 15 parties. Only SGP (3 seats), DENK (3 seats), and Volt (1 seat, sole MP) have zero women. Compared to 2023 — when four parties sent all-male delegations including FvD, JA21, DENK, and SGP — this represents structural progress.

The key structural change is the collapse of PVV's dominance. In the 2023 term, PVV's 37 seats and 19% female share structurally depressed Kamer-wide speaking averages, particularly in the asylum and migration debates that dominated 2024-25. With PVV now at 26 seats (and effectively 19 after the Groep-Markuszower split in January 2026), this structural drag is reduced.

D66's rise is the most significant gender story of 2025. From 9 seats (5 women, 56%) to 26 seats (13 women, 50%) — it is now both the joint-largest party and a majority-female delegation. With Rob Jetten as PM and D66 anchoring the Jetten cabinet, the party that leads the government is also one with near-parity internal composition. Whether this translates to speaking time parity is the question the 2025 Handelingen data will answer.

The 2023-term Handelingen analysis documented a consistent topic segregation: women spoke above their 39.3% seat share in health, education, and social affairs, and well below it in defence, foreign affairs, and asylum. The asylum debate was the most imbalanced: PVV's 81% male delegation drove floor time in the politically dominant topic of 2024-25.

The 2025 term (from November 2025) is still early — full Handelingen analysis is forthcoming. But the structural conditions for change are present. The dominant topic has shifted: asylum has given way to European defence, housing, and climate. The parties most associated with those topics (D66, GL-PvdA, CDA) have higher female representation. The compositional engine driving speaking segregation in 2023 is smaller.

Two early signals from 2025-term plenary records (November 2025 – April 2026) suggest the gap is narrowing. In defence debates — historically the most male-dominated — Dilan Yeşilgöz (VVD) as minister and the cabinet's European defence focus have elevated female voices on a topic that was previously male-dominated by PVV and Schoof-era ministers. In housing debates, the topic remains female-led but now with more prominence.

The topic segregation hypothesis for 2025: women will continue to lead on health, education, and social affairs. The question is whether European defence — now the politically central topic under Jetten — follows the pattern of the 2023 asylum debates (male-dominated) or begins to shift as the minister herself is female. The Handelingen data will answer this in the coming months.

The Suburban Swing

How the space between city and countryside became the most consequential — and restless — zone in Western democracy.

By Evgeniya Orlova  ·  April 2025  ·  US · NL · FR · IT
01 — Introduction

The geography of discontent

In election after election across the Western world, the same pattern has emerged: cities lean left, rural areas lean right, and the suburbs — that vast, sprawling middle ground — go whichever way the political wind is blowing. But to call suburbs merely "swing" territory understates their significance. They are, increasingly, the engine of democratic change.

This report examines suburban voting patterns across four major Western democracies — the United States (presidential election, 2024), the Netherlands (parliamentary elections, October 29 2025), France (legislative elections 2024 and presidential election 2022), and Italy (parliamentary elections 2022). Drawing on electoral geography data, it tests two hypotheses about why suburbs swing.

Hypothesis 1

Suburbs are structurally volatile

Unlike cities (stable left) or rural areas (stable right), suburbs contain the socio-economic mix that makes them responsive to immediate material concerns over ideological loyalty.

✓ Confirmed across all cases
Hypothesis 2

Right-wing parties capture suburban anxieties more effectively

Right-wing populist and conservative parties have outperformed in suburbs by addressing cost of living, safety, housing, and immigration — quintessentially suburban concerns.

The suburban voter is not voting against democracy. They are voting against the price of a litre of milk, a mortgage renewal, and a 45-minute commute on a road that hasn't been resurfaced since 2009.

The Dutch exception — what it tells us

The 2025 Netherlands election introduces a crucial nuance. D66's surge showed that suburban voters can be pulled back from the far right when a credible alternative contests their material concerns — above all, housing. But D66 did not win the suburbs; it won the cities. Almere, Capelle, and Maastricht all stayed with PVV even as Rotterdam and The Hague flipped. The far-right total (PVV + JA21 + FvD = 42 seats) actually grew. This is the key analytical finding: suburban swing operates primarily within the right bloc, not between left and right.

04 — Conclusions

Who owns the suburb?

For now, the right — but cities remain reliably left or centre-left across all four cases. Amsterdam voted GL-PvdA + D66 at over 50%. Paris gave RN 14%, with NFP winning 12 of 18 seats. Bologna and Turin's city centres stayed with PD even as their hinterlands moved right. This urban-suburban polarity is the defining electoral geography of the 2020s.

The suburb's defining characteristic is volatility, conditional on material outcomes. The Netherlands 2025 case is the strongest proof: the same suburban electorate that gave PVV a landslide in 2023 punished it in 2025 — while still not moving left. Whether centre-left parties can reclaim the suburb depends on whether they can rebuild a vocabulary of material concern that speaks to the périurbain, the Randstad commuter belt, and the Roman hinterland. So far, they have not.

The energy crisis ballot: how gas price spikes in 2022 reshaped Italian voters' priorities — and why the wrong party won on the issue

Household energy costs rose 131% in a single year. Voters ranked the crisis as their top concern. Yet the election went to a party that had nothing to do with resolving it. This is the story of how economic pain became an anti-establishment signal — and what that means for policy.

Election: Sept 25, 2022 Sources: ARERA · ITANES · CISE/ICCP · IEA Issue: Energy · Purchasing Power
```

The crisis in numbers

To understand what Italian voters faced in September 2022, start with the bills. According to ARERA, the Italian energy regulator, household electricity costs rose 94% in Q1 2022 compared to the same quarter in 2021. Gas jumped 131% in the same period. These were not marginal increases absorbed by household savings — they hit at the worst possible moment: after two years of pandemic-related income disruption, with inflation climbing to 9%, its highest in decades.

The IEA's quarterly price index shows that Italian gas import prices peaked in Q3 2022 at six times their 2018 baseline — a price level without precedent in the post-war energy market. Consumer group Codacons estimated that the average Italian household would spend €4,724 on energy alone in 2023, roughly €2,500 more than in 2021.

A Bank of Italy study (Curci et al., 2022) found that government mitigation measures — including VAT reductions on gas, suspended system charges, and a one-off €200 social bonus for lower-income workers — had only "limited effect" on purchasing power disparities. Low-income households were disproportionately exposed because they lived in less energy-efficient housing and used older, more energy-intensive appliances. The poor paid more — and the state's response was insufficient to close the gap.

The political trigger: how the crisis brought down the government

The energy crisis did not just shape voter concern — it directly caused the election to happen. The Draghi government fell in July 2022 precisely because of a dispute over a cost-of-living package. On July 14, the Five Star Movement (M5S) — led by former Prime Minister Giuseppe Conte — boycotted a Senate confidence vote on the decreto aiuti (Economic Aid Decree), a wide-ranging bill aimed at helping households and businesses cope with surging energy costs. The decree also contained a controversial waste incinerator for Rome, which M5S opposed. But the broader conflict was Conte's sustained criticism of Draghi's approach to the M5S's flagship welfare programmes — the Reddito di Cittadinanza (basic income) and the Superbonus 110% (building energy efficiency tax credit).

"The period we are going through doesn't allow for any pause in determining interventions to contrast the economic and social crisis, and in particular the increase in inflation."

President Sergio Mattarella, July 21, 2022, accepting Draghi's resignation and calling the snap election

The collapse was, paradoxically, triggered by a bill meant to help people cope with the energy crisis. M5S — the party most associated with welfare protection — pulled down the government as it tried to pass emergency energy relief. This created the snap election in which energy would be the dominant issue. The contradiction set the terms for everything that followed.

What voters actually said: the ITANES data

The 2022 Italian National Election Study (ITANES) — a rolling cross-section pre-electoral survey of 6,250 respondents followed by a post-electoral panel of 4,696 — provides the clearest picture of what voters prioritised going into September 25. The answer is unambiguous: economic issues dominated. Unemployment and the energy crisis ranked as the most important concerns. Immigration, which had led issue salience rankings in 2018, dropped significantly. The war in Ukraine arrived in February 2022 and its economic consequences — particularly on energy costs — became, in the words of one academic analysis using ITANES data, "the top priority in the minds of the voters."

The CISE/ICCP 2022 pre-electoral survey (35 issues, ~2,600 respondents) adds texture. Italian voters in 2022 held broadly progressive economic stances: they favoured a minimum wage, opposed the flat tax, supported a wealth tax on assets above €5 million, and broadly endorsed the basic income concept (while being dissatisfied with its implementation). On energy specifically, the electorate was not divided on whether the crisis needed addressing — it was a valence issue, where all parties agreed action was needed and the debate was over which action.

The paradox: progressive electorate, right-wing outcome

Here is the core analytical puzzle. ITANES and CISE/ICCP data both show an electorate with progressive economic preferences and an energy crisis as its dominant concern. The election result was the worst performance of the Italian left in its history and a landslide for the centre-right coalition — specifically for Fratelli d'Italia (FdI), which surged from 4% in 2018 to 26% in 2022.

How? The answer comes in two parts.

First, the opposition was structurally incapable of capturing the energy issue. Every major party had been inside the Draghi "national unity" government — PD, M5S, Lega, Forza Italia — and therefore shared responsibility for its energy crisis response. The only major party that had maintained consistent opposition was FdI. This gave Meloni a unique position: she could criticise the government's energy response without being implicated in it. She could promise continuity on fiscal discipline (reassuring markets) while campaigning against incumbents (mobilising voters).

Second, and more structurally significant: FdI's success was driven not by policy alignment but by leader evaluation. The ITANES-based study by Puleo, Carteny and Piccolino (2025) is decisive here. Using logistic regression on ITANES 2022 individual-level data, they find that the primary predictor of switching to FdI was a positive evaluation of Giorgia Meloni as a leader. This held across different voter origin groups. Among voters switching from Lega to FdI, Meloni approval was the sole significant predictor. Among M5S switchers, issue preferences added some explanatory power — but Meloni's personal approval dominated.

Central Finding — Italy

The energy crisis was the dominant issue in 2022 by salience. But it did not produce issue-aligned voting. Because energy was a valence issue (everyone agreed it was bad; all parties promised to fix it), it offered no positional advantage to any party. Instead, it amplified a general anti-incumbent signal. That signal was captured by the one major party outside government — FdI — and personalised through Meloni's leadership approval rating. Economic pain drove voters away from the parties most associated with managing the crisis, and toward the only opposition leader who had maintained a credible outsider position throughout.

The left did not lose on energy policy. It lost because it owned the government that failed on energy — and the right owned the only credible opposition figure.

The vote-flow evidence

The CISE/ICCP city-level vote-flow analysis (Turin, Florence, Naples) shows the texture of this shift. FdI drew from across the party spectrum: approximately 38% of its 2022 voters had voted Lega in 2018; 20% came from Forza Italia; 16.7% from M5S; and notably 11.2% from PD — the centre-left. This cross-cutting draw is the signature of a valence-issue election, where no single ideological bloc moved cleanly. The energy crisis created a generalised "throw them out" sentiment that FdI — as the only major party outside government — was positioned to absorb.

Turnout also tells part of the story. At 63.9%, it was the lowest in republican Italian history — 9 percentage points below 2018. The energy crisis generated anger, but the collapse of the Draghi coalition also generated disillusionment. Among those who abstained, many were likely former M5S voters who felt their party had both betrayed its welfare promises and destroyed the one government capable of managing the crisis. This abstention disproportionately weakened the centre-left.

What this means for policy interpretation

For a beleidsmedewerker reading this result, the key lesson is about issue ownership in valence contexts. When an issue like energy costs becomes a universal concern — where no party can credibly claim a distinctive solution — it stops being a policy issue and becomes a performance signal. Voters stop asking "which party has the better energy plan?" and start asking "which party has been least responsible for the mess?" In 2022, that question had only one answer: the party that had never been in government.

This has implications for how governments communicate in crises. The Draghi government did, by most technical accounts, manage the energy crisis competently — it secured alternative gas supplies, suspended system charges, introduced social bonuses. But the political cost of governing through the crisis, being inside a coalition with parties that then sabotaged it, and failing to claim narrative ownership of the response, was total.
```

🇫🇷 France — June 30 / July 7, 2024

Snap elections, issue salience and the NFP: what the 2024 dissolution actually measured — and why voters punished Macron on procedure, not policy

Purchasing power was the top voter concern. Immigration was second. But the 2024 snap election campaign was not fought on either. Researchers found no policy issue was salient during the campaign itself — it was about fear, democratic procedure, and the legitimacy of the dissolution. The data tells a more complicated story.

Election: June 30 / July 7, 2024 Sources: Ipsos-CEVIPOF · Talan/Ipsos · West European Politics Issue: Dissolution · Purchasing Power · 49.3
```

The trigger: one hour after the results came in

On the evening of June 9, 2024, one hour after European Parliament election results were announced, President Emmanuel Macron made a surprise televised address and dissolved the National Assembly. The trigger was the scale of the RN's European victory: 31.4% of the French vote, against Ensemble's 14.6%. It was, as researchers would later document, a "crisis dissolution" — in the classification of French constitutional scholars, the rarest and most dangerous kind.

The timing was described by almost every political analyst in France as a shock. Even those who had anticipated a possible autumn dissolution were unprepared for the immediacy. In less than 48 hours: the left formed the Nouveau Front Populaire (NFP — LFI, PS, Greens, PCF); LR president Éric Ciotti allied his party with RN; and candidates had to register within six days of the dissolution under French electoral law.

What voters said they cared about: the Ipsos data

The question of what drove the 2024 vote must start with what voters said mattered to them — and then confront the uncomfortable gap between that and what the campaign was actually about.

The Ipsos-CEVIPOF-Le Monde-FJJ-Institut Montaigne panel study, which tracked over 10,000 participants across four waves in the 12 months before the June 2024 European elections, provides the baseline. For the EU elections — the event that triggered the dissolution — purchasing power ranked first among decisive issues at 47% of voters. Immigration followed at 38%, healthcare at 29%. France's role in Europe came fifth at just 20%.

For the snap legislative election itself, a Talan/Ipsos survey conducted in late June 2024 asked voters which issues would most determine their vote. Purchasing power retained its lead: 59% of respondents named it as one of their three most decisive issues. Immigration was cited by over a third. Unemployment, strikingly, was mentioned by only 5%.

The gap: what the campaign was actually fought on

Here is the analytical crux. Academic researchers studying the 2024 French legislative elections (Gougou & Persico, West European Politics, 2024) found something striking: "No public policy issue was salient in the campaign, with the debate focusing on the government's record and the RN's ability to secure an absolute majority in the National Assembly in order to govern."

This is a significant finding. Voters told pollsters they cared most about purchasing power. But the campaign — shaped by the three-week sprint from dissolution to first round — was not about purchasing power policy. It was about fear: fear of an RN government, fear of institutional breakdown, and the question of whether Macron's dissolution was a legitimate democratic act or an autocratic gamble.

Why the gap? Three reasons. First, the compressed timeline (three weeks from dissolution to first round) made it structurally impossible for parties to build issue-based campaigns. Second, the NFP campaign made no clear prime ministerial candidate — it was explicitly a blocking coalition, not a governing programme. Third, the Ensemble campaign was led by PM Attal and focused on attacking "the extremes." The issue agenda was crowded out by constitutional drama.

The pension reform antecedent: procedure as the real grievance

To understand why procedural legitimacy dominated the 2024 campaign, you have to go back to March 2023. Macron's government used Article 49.3 — the constitutional mechanism allowing a bill to pass without a parliamentary vote unless a no-confidence motion passes — to push through the pension reform raising the retirement age from 62 to 64. The move was technically legal; 49.3 had been used approximately 100 times since 1958. But this use was qualitatively different: it forced through a deeply unpopular structural reform over explicit parliamentary and public opposition.

The polling damage was severe. An Elabe poll in March 2023 found that 68% of respondents wanted the no-confidence motion to pass. 69% believed the use of 49.3 was "a denial of democracy." Macron's approval rating fell to 28% — as low as during the gilets jaunes protests of 2018-2019. A Journal du Dimanche poll put him at his lowest point in four years.

"82% of the French public disapproves of Macron's use of Article 49.3 — including a majority of those voters who supported him in the first round of the 2022 presidential election."

Foreign Policy analysis, May 2023, citing Elabe polling data

The pension reform created a durable grievance that was not about pension ages per se — it was about the method of governance. Voters who supported raising the retirement age in principle still disapproved of bypassing parliament to do so. This procedural anger was the background radiation of French politics in 2024. When Macron dissolved parliament on European election night — itself a dramatic unilateral executive act — it reactivated the same procedural grievance in voters who had learned not to trust his instincts about democratic legitimacy.

What the result measured

The June 30 / July 7 results produced a hung parliament. NFP won a plurality of 182 seats (Le Monde analysis). Ensemble followed with 168. RN, despite leading in first-round vote share at 29.3%, finished third at 143 seats — blocked by the "republican front" dynamic documented by Ipsos exit data, which showed 72% of NFP first-round voters backing Ensemble candidates over RN in second-round duels.

What did this measure? Not purchasing power preferences — the top-ranked voter concern never became a policy battleground. Not immigration — both RN and NFP pledged to reverse the pension reform and made broad spending commitments, but the campaign did not turn on specific policy platforms. What it measured, primarily, was two things: the strength of anti-RN tactical voting (the republican front) and the depth of anti-Macron punishment among voters who blamed him for procedural overreach.

Central Finding — France

The 2024 snap election was not won or lost on purchasing power, even though purchasing power was voters' top stated concern. It was fought and decided on procedural legitimacy — the accumulated anger at Macron's governing style, activated by the dissolution decision itself. The pension reform's 49.3 bypass was the original trauma; the dissolution was its repetition. Voters punished Macron not for a specific policy failure but for repeated demonstrations that he would use institutional power to circumvent opposition.

For policy advisors, the lesson is that process is policy. A government can make defensible policy decisions and still face electoral punishment if the method of implementation signals contempt for democratic deliberation. The Barnier government's fall in December 2024 — again via 49.3 on the budget — confirmed that the lesson had not been learned.

The NFP paradox: a blocking coalition that accidentally won

The NFP did not run as a governing coalition. It ran as a firewall against RN. Its formation — within 48 hours of the dissolution — was a tactical emergency, not an ideological convergence. LFI and PS had fundamental disagreements (on Ukraine, on Israel-Palestine, on economic policy) that had already broken the previous NUPES alliance. The coalition had no prime ministerial candidate and no shared programme beyond reversing the pension reform and opposing RN.

Yet it won the most seats. The republican front — the tactical withdrawal of candidates to maximise the anti-RN vote in second rounds — worked. Ipsos data showed Ensemble first-round voters backing NFP candidates over RN by 54%-15% in triangular contests. The result was not a mandate for the NFP programme (there was no coherent programme) but a vote against the alternative.

Macron's subsequent refusal to appoint NFP candidate Lucie Castets as prime minister — choosing instead LR's Michel Barnier — completed the circle of procedural grievance. The election had been called to force a democratic choice. When the choice produced an inconvenient result, it was overridden. The Barnier government fell after 90 days, toppled by the same NFP-RN combination, on a 49.3 usage. France entered 2025 with its fifth prime minister in three years.
```

🇳🇱 Netherlands — November 22, 2023

Housing crisis as electoral issue: why affordability dominated the 2023 campaign — and why the party that won had the thinnest housing plan

87% of Dutch voters said there was a housing crisis. Housing was the top priority for a new cabinet. But the Dutch Parliamentary Election Study confirmed migration was the largest concern among voters on election day. And the party with two pages on housing in a 40-page manifesto won 37 seats. This is a story about issue displacement, bandwagon effects, and the failure of the left to convert economic anger into votes.

Election: November 22, 2023 Sources: CBS · DPES · Aedes/Ipsos I&O · ABN AMRO · ABF Research Issue: Housing · Issue Ownership · PVV surge
```

The crisis in numbers

The Dutch housing crisis going into the 2023 election was structural, multi-decade, and worsening. ABF Research's annual model-based estimate, calculated for the Ministry of the Interior, showed the housing shortage rising from 315,000 in 2022 to 390,000 by mid-2023 — on a trajectory that ABF projected would reach 450,000 by 2027 without major intervention. By 2024, CBS (Statistics Netherlands) confirmed the shortage at approximately 400,000 homes, representing 5% of the total housing stock.

The numbers behind the shortage illuminate who was affected. CBS data shows that 20.8% of Dutch people aged 25–29 were still living with their parents on January 1, 2024 — and the trend was upward. In 2023, young households (under 24) numbered one million, a 21% increase since 2014; 65% of people aged 18–24 were living at home. Transaction volume for student and youth housing fell 58% between 2022 and 2023. Rent levels were 23% higher in 2024 than in 2015. Home purchase costs rose 25% between 2020 and 2023, and 60% since 2015.

Private sector renters faced the sharpest pressure. CBS data shows that renters — especially in the private (unregulated) sector — had the highest ratio of housing costs to disposable income, well above owner-occupiers. The Netherlands ranked among the worst in the EU-27 on this indicator: only Germany, Denmark, Sweden, and Greece had higher ratios. This was not a marginal affordability problem — it was a structural distributional failure, concentrated among young people, single-person households, and lower-income renters.

Housing as an electoral issue: persistent but never owned

ABN AMRO's election housing monitor makes an important historical observation: housing has been "a key election topic since 1981" in the Netherlands. It has appeared in every party manifesto in every election for over four decades. For two thirds of voters, it is reportedly the issue on which they most determine their vote. Yet it has never produced issue ownership — no single party has successfully claimed to be the credible solver of the housing crisis.

The 2023 pre-election Aedes/Ipsos I&O survey (N=2,000+) confirmed housing as the dominant concern: it was named by the highest share of respondents as the priority for the new cabinet, above any other topic. 87% felt there was a housing market crisis. Yet the manifestos of the major parties offered largely undifferentiated responses — varied mainly by market vs. regulation philosophy, not by commitment or credibility:

D66 pledged to build 10 new cities. CDA promised 100,000 homes per year. PVV offered a 10% cut in social rents, simplified planning rules, and scrapped environmental sustainability requirements — its housing chapter ran two pages in a 40-page manifesto. GL-PvdA proposed stricter rent regulation and more social housing. No party's plan was considered detailed or financially credible by analysts.

"The main problem this year is the same as so often: there are not enough affordable homes. For two-thirds of voters, this is the topic on which they largely determine their vote."

ABN AMRO Housing Market Monitor, Dutch Elections Edition, 2023

What actually happened during the campaign: migration displaced housing

The 2023 election was triggered in July by the collapse of the Rutte IV coalition — specifically over disagreements on asylum and migration policy between the VVD and D66. The campaign was therefore framed from its opening around immigration. Geert Wilders — who had toned down his most extreme positions and was newly rendered "coalition-eligible" by VVD leader Dilan Yeşilgöz's statement that she would no longer exclude PVV from coalition talks — ran a campaign almost entirely focused on immigration restriction.

The Dutch Parliamentary Election Study (DPES 2023, involving 38 political scientists and sociologists) confirmed what polls showed in the final weeks: migration had become the largest voter concern by election day. Housing, which dominated surveys in October, was displaced. The DPES found that a third of the electorate changed their favoured party during the campaign — including across different ideological blocs — indicating extraordinary volatility in the final weeks.

The DPES pre-election wave data reveals the bandwagon mechanism: 10% of respondents shifted from another party to the PVV between their pre-election stated intention and their actual vote. An additional 5% voted PVV having been undecided before the election. These late-moving groups were larger than the 8% who had named PVV in both pre- and post-election surveys — meaning the PVV's final result was substantially driven by last-minute switchers, not a stable base.

Why the left lost on housing

The most analytically significant result of 2023 was not the PVV victory. It was the failure of the Dutch left to convert housing anger into votes. GL-PvdA, running under former European Commission vice president Frans Timmermans, had the most detailed housing and cost-of-living platform among major parties. It finished second with 25 seats — a solid result, but far from converting the 87% who said there was a housing crisis into an electoral majority.

The Institute for New Economic Thinking's post-election analysis (Haffner & Boumeester) identifies the structural cause: "The Dutch Left failed to win on what used to be its ideological home turf. A considerable number of voters who had supported the Socialist Party and the Labour Party in 2021 now voted for the PVV or NSC." Housing distress, cost-of-living anger, and economic precarity — traditionally left terrain — were captured by the populist right.

Central Finding — Netherlands

Housing was the dominant voter concern before the campaign. But the campaign displaced it with migration — the issue that triggered the election — and Wilders was the issue owner on migration. The left's failure was not programmatic: GL-PvdA had better housing policy. It was a failure of issue framing. The left could not prevent migration from dominating when the election had been triggered by a migration crisis in the coalition, and when Wilders had successfully connected housing shortage to immigration as a single narrative ("immigrants take your homes").

CBS data and Dutch refugee council figures contradict this framing: in 2022, approximately 7% of newly available social housing was allocated to refugee households (roughly 11,000 units) compared to 150,000 allocated to non-refugee households. The housing crisis was not caused by immigration. But the narrative — effective enough to shift 15% of the electorate in the campaign's final weeks — did not need to be true to be decisive.

The Wilders connection: housing + immigration as a single issue

Wilders' political insight was to merge two separate crises into one causal story. The housing shortage was real. Immigration was visible. Connecting them — regardless of the statistical relationship — gave voters who were frustrated with housing costs a single culprit and a single solution. His manifesto's housing chapter promised a 10% social rent cut and simplified planning (popular among those waiting for social housing) while simultaneously promising to halt asylum seekers (popular among those who blamed immigration for the wait).

Atlantic Council analysis notes that 78% of eligible Dutch voters believed the presence of refugees made it harder to find housing — a remarkable figure for a claim not supported by the data. The narrative penetration was near-total. The left's response — factual rebuttals about the actual allocation of social housing — proved no match for a story that felt intuitively true to people sleeping in their cars or on years-long social housing waiting lists.

What housing did and didn't change

The PVV-led coalition (PVV, VVD, NSC, BBB) that formed after the 2023 election did not significantly advance Dutch housing policy before collapsing in June 2024. The Affordable Rent Act — introduced in July 2024, under the Schoof government — was a regulatory intervention that actually reduced the private rental market segment, potentially exacerbating future shortages, according to OECD analysis. The housing crisis continued to worsen: the shortage rose from 390,000 in 2023 to 400,000+ by 2024.

For the 2025 election campaign — triggered by PVV's withdrawal from the coalition over asylum policy — housing again ranked as a leading voter concern. The cycle repeated: housing salience, migration framing, housing unresolved.
```
