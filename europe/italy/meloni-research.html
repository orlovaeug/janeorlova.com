01 — Origin & Rise

From Garbatella to Palazzo Chigi

Giorgia Meloni was born on January 15, 1977, and raised in Garbatella — a working-class district in central Rome — after her father abandoned the family when she was a child. At just 15, she joined the Italian Social Movement (MSI), a neo-fascist party founded by followers of Mussolini. This was not a brief youthful phase; she openly praised Mussolini in her teens and remained a visible presence in post-fascist political circles throughout her formative years.

Timeline
1992
Joins the Far-Right Youth Front
At 15, joins the Youth Front of the Italian Social Movement (MSI), a neo-fascist party. She later leads its student wing, Student Action, and becomes a defining figure in Italy's post-fascist circles.
1998–2002
Provincial Councillor, Rome
Elected to the provincial government of Rome at just 21, beginning her formal political career. She then becomes president of the youth wing of the National Alliance (AN), the MSI's successor party.
2006
Elected to Parliament
Wins a seat in the Chamber of Deputies. Two years later, Berlusconi appoints her Minister for Youth Policies at age 31 — the youngest minister in postwar Italian history.
2012
Co-founds Brothers of Italy (FdI)
After Berlusconi's government collapses, she co-founds Fratelli d'Italia (Brothers of Italy), the FdI logo famously incorporating the tricolor flame — a symbol of the old fascist MSI. The party wins just 1.96% in 2013.
2014–2021
Years in Opposition
While all other major parties cycle through coalition governments, FdI remains the only party in consistent opposition throughout this period. Its vote share climbs steadily: 3.67% (2014 EU elections), 4.33% (2018 generals), 6.46% (2019 EU elections). This outsider status becomes her biggest political asset.
July 2022
Mario Draghi's Government Collapses
The national unity coalition, propped up by all major parties, falls apart. Snap elections are called for September. As the only major opposition leader, Meloni is perfectly positioned. Her coalition partners — Salvini's League and Berlusconi's Forza Italia — agree she leads the ticket.
September 25, 2022
Election Victory — 26% of the Vote
In a record-low turnout election (64.67%), Brothers of Italy wins 26% — up from 4% just four years prior. The right-wing coalition takes 44.43% of the vote. Meloni tells supporters: "Italy has chosen us, and we won't betray her."
October 22, 2022
Takes Office as Prime Minister
Sworn in as Italy's 68th postwar government head and its first female prime minister. She vows to deliver "a Copernican revolution" in fiscal policy and "free the best energies of this nation," even if that means clashing with elites.
02 — The Factors Behind Her Landslide

Why Italy Chose Her

Meloni's rise from 2% to 26% in under a decade was not a fluke. It was the product of structural political conditions, smart strategic choices, and her opponents' failures — all converging at the same moment.

🗳️
The Only Consistent Opposition
Every other major party — the Left, the Five Star Movement, even Salvini's League — participated in some form of coalition government between 2018 and 2022. FdI was the sole holdout. By 2022, the electorate identified her as the only credible choice for real change. Opposition purity was her most valuable asset.
Key Factor
🏚️
A Shattered Political Landscape
Italy had seen four different coalition governments in the five years before her election. Decades of instability, economic stagnation, corruption scandals and the trauma of COVID fostered deep voter distrust. FdI absorbed protest votes the way the Five Star Movement had in 2013 and 2018.
Key Factor
🤝
Coalition Unity vs. Left's Fragmentation
The right-wing coalition (FdI, League, Forza Italia) presented a united front. Meanwhile, the centre-left Democratic Party explicitly ruled out an alliance with the Five Star Movement, and the moderate "Third Pole" split the remaining vote. Electoral rules heavily rewarded the largest coalition — ensuring a parliamentary supermajority.
Key Factor
📢
Moderate Rebranding & Communication
During the campaign, Meloni softened her Euroskeptic rhetoric, pledged to support Ukraine and NATO, and deliberately distanced herself from her coalition partners' pro-Russian sympathies. She downplayed FdI's neo-fascist roots and framed herself as a mainstream conservative under the motto "God, country, and family." Her opponents focused on her history — a strategy that proved ineffective.
Key Factor

The most striking trust moment of her tenure came in April 2026, when Trump publicly attacked Meloni after Italy refused to back the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran and she defended the newly elected Pope Leo XIV. A poll by Noto Poll found 81% of Italians backed her position — with support cutting across all party lines, including 89% of centre-left Democratic Party voters. The dispute paradoxically strengthened her domestically even as it strained a relationship she had carefully cultivated for years.

04 — Policy Record

Three Years of Governing

Meloni came to office promising radical change. The reality has been far more pragmatic — defined more by what she did not do (blow up Italy's finances, confront Brussels, abandon Ukraine) than by sweeping transformation. Her strongest critics say she has been a cautious caretaker. Her defenders point to stability, deficit reduction, and reshaping the EU on migration.

📉
Fiscal Policy — Unexpected Orthodoxy
Meloni's biggest surprise. She halved Italy's budget deficit from 8% to under 3% of GDP, earned a Fitch credit rating upgrade in September 2025, and reduced the Italian-German bond spread to 16-year lows. Ambitious flat-tax promises were largely watered down. Italy's industrial output fell 7.5% over three years; GDP grew just 0.5% in 2025. Critics note that much of what growth there was came from EU pandemic recovery funds (NRRP) inherited from Draghi, set to expire in 2026.
Mixed Record
⛵
Immigration — Numbers Down, Battles Up
Illegal sea arrivals dropped from 157,651 in 2023 to 66,317 in 2024 — a ~60% reduction. Bilateral deals with Tunisia (€1.3bn EU deal) and the "Mattei Plan for Africa" reshaped EU migration policy. The headline Albania offshore processing deal, however, was largely struck down by Italian and EU courts as violating international refugee protections. Paradoxically, the government issued 450,000+ work visas (2023–25) and plans 500,000 more (2026–28) to meet Italy's labour needs.
Partial Success
🌍
Foreign Policy — Italy's Global Repositioning
Italy under Meloni shifted from a second-tier EU voice to a central one. She maintained strong Ukraine support despite coalition partners' pro-Russian sympathies, brokered Italy as a transatlantic bridge, and was ranked the most powerful person in Europe by Politico in 2025. Her attendance at Trump's second inauguration gave Italy rare U.S. access — later strained by disputes over Iran and the Pope in April 2026. She also launched the "Mattei Plan for Africa," visiting the continent 14 times in three years.
Notable Achievement
⚖️
Justice Reform — Biggest Domestic Setback
Meloni personally championed the "Nordio Reform" — a constitutional overhaul to separate judges from prosecutors and restructure the High Council of the Judiciary. It was put to a referendum in March 2026 and failed, briefly sparking speculation of a snap election or reshuffles. Several ministers resigned in the aftermath (Tourism Minister Santanchè, Justice Undersecretary Delmastro). She absorbed the blow and pressed on without a formal reshuffle.
Major Setback
🏠
Housing & Social Policy (2026)
In late April 2026, eyeing the 2027 election, Meloni's cabinet launched a housing access programme aiming to make 100,000+ homes available over 10 years. Tax incentives for families with 3+ children, modest wage measures in the 2026 budget (~€18 billion), and selective healthcare spending increases round out a pre-election domestic agenda.
In Progress
📺
Media & Institutions — A Contested Record
Meloni sought a constitutional reform ("premierato") to allow direct election of the prime minister — stalled in parliament. Her government moved to replace top officials at Italy's public broadcaster RAI and was publicly reprimanded by the European Commission for restricting media independence. Critics also cite her government's conflicts with the judiciary as attempts at institutional capture.
Criticized
05 — Policy Evolution

How Her Positions Changed

One of the most analytically interesting aspects of Meloni's tenure is the gap between her campaign persona and her governing reality — and then the gradual drift back toward her original instincts. Analysts at Foreign Affairs described a "distinctively incremental, nonconfrontational pivot to the right" after she secured international credibility.

06 — Vision & What Comes Next

The Final Year and Beyond

With elections constitutionally due by spring 2027, Meloni enters what analysts describe as a "challenging final year." She remains the favourite to win re-election, but faces a narrowing fiscal window, expiring EU recovery funds, U.S. tariff pressure on Italy's export economy, and a newly energised opposition beginning to coalesce around a primary process.

I
The Longevity Record — September 4, 2026
Her most personal political goal: surpassing Berlusconi's record of 1,412 continuous days in office (set 2001–2005). On September 4, 2026, if she is still in office, she becomes Italy's longest-serving postwar prime minister. She has stated this as a marker of her core promise — restoring "political stability rare in the history of the Republic." She is also aiming to be the first modern Italian leader to complete a full five-year term without reshuffles or new confidence votes.
II
Housing & Cost-of-Living Agenda
Her government unveiled a housing plan in April 2026 aiming to make 100,000+ homes available over 10 years — explicitly framed as an election-year push to address cost-of-living anxieties. With EU recovery funds drying up in 2026 and the budget deficit limiting spending, Meloni's fiscal room is narrow. She has sketched a €18 billion budget for 2026 centred on modest tax cuts, family benefits, and wage measures for lower earners.
III
EU Influence & Migration Expansion
Meloni's stated ambition is to "do in Europe what we did in Italy" — reshape European politics from inside institutions rather than by confronting them. She aims to maintain ECR leadership in the European Parliament and build cross-group coalitions on issues like immigration externalisation, Green Deal rollback, and EU budget reform. The bloc is moving in her direction on migration, even as her Albania deal faltered.
IV
Premierato: Direct Election of Prime Minister
Meloni's most ambitious constitutional vision — allowing Italian voters to directly elect the prime minister — remains stalled. She frames it as the natural conclusion of a 30-year trend toward leader-centric politics in Italy. Even if it cannot pass before 2027, it is expected to form a centrepiece of her next election manifesto, positioning her as the candidate for a reformed, more stable Italy.
V
The 2027 Election — Favourite, But Not Certain
Analysts including Federico Santi of Eurasia Group describe Meloni as "the likely favourite to win the next election," but warn: "this is Italy and it doesn't take much for the winds to change." Her coalition holds more support in 2026 polls than at the time of their 2022 victory — a rare achievement for a sitting government. The centre-left opposition has begun organising a primary process, but remains fragmented. The decisive factors will be Italy's economic performance, Trump's continuing unpredictability, and whether her final year delivers visible improvements in living standards.

"Meloni has kept Italy stable, but has not changed its trajectory. The country's structural weaknesses remain: low productivity, high public debt, and limited investment in the future."

— Marina Cino Pagliarello, LSE EUROPP, October 2025

The core paradox of Meloni's tenure is this: she has achieved something extraordinary — durable political stability in the most unstable democracy in Western Europe — without resolving the underlying economic fragility that makes that stability so necessary. Whether she can convert longevity into transformation, and stability into legacy, is the question that the next twelve months will begin to answer.
