06 · Why People Vote AfD

KEY VOTER MOTIVATIONS

🚫
Immigration
Anti-immigration remains the AfD's signature issue. The party advocates strict border controls and deportations. 41% of young Germans (14–29) cited immigration as a top concern in 2024 studies.
#1 Issue
💸
Economic Anxiety
Cost-of-living pressures, energy costs, and deindustrialization hit eastern Germany hardest. AfD surged amid the collapse of the SPD-led coalition and growing mainstream discontent.
#2 Issue
🗳️
Protest / Veto Vote
Over 100,000 voters switched from Die Linke to AfD in 2025 — a "veto vote" against the establishment. The party has never been in federal government, making it a credible anti-system choice.
~30–40% of voters
🏛️
EU Scepticism
AfD advocates reducing EU competences and returning power to nation-states. Alice Weidel has called for dismantling the EU "superstructure." The party aligns with Orbán's Hungary in the EP.
Core Policy
📱
Social Media Reach
Weidel's TikTok and YouTube presence attracted young voters. The new "Generation Deutschland" youth wing launched in late 2025 institutionalizes this digital outreach strategy.
Youth Driver
🧭
Eastern Identity
A distinct post-reunification identity plays a major role in the east. Eastern voters perceive themselves as economically and culturally "left behind." The AfD frames itself as their voice.
East Factor
07 · Historical Timeline

THE AfD TRAJECTORY

2013 — Founded
4.7%
Founded by economists opposing euro bailouts. Narrowly missed the 5% threshold in its first federal election. Original focus: euro scepticism, not immigration.
2017 — Enters Bundestag
12.6%
Capitalizing on the 2015 refugee crisis, AfD entered the Bundestag as the third-largest party with 94 seats. Leadership pivoted decisively toward immigration and national identity.
2021 — Slips to 5th Place
10.4%
Lost 2.3 percentage points, fell to fifth place. SPD won under Scholz; Greens surged. AfD base remained in the East but appeared to plateau nationally.
2024 — State Election Breakthroughs
32.8% (Thuringia)
AfD became the first far-right party to win a state election plurality since the Nazi era — taking first in Thuringia, Saxony, and Brandenburg. Three eastern branches classified as "definitely extremist."
Feb 2025 — Federal Election Record
20.8%
AfD doubled its federal vote share to 20.8% — its best-ever national result. Became 2nd-largest party with 151 seats. Won almost all direct constituencies in eastern Germany. CDU refused coalition; the Brandmauer held.
May 2025 — Designated Extremist
Federal intelligence agency BfV classified the whole AfD as a "confirmed right-wing extremist" organization in a 1,100-page report. AfD called the designation politically motivated.
Late 2025 — Surpasses CDU in Polls
~26–27%
AfD recorded its highest-ever national figures, surpassing CDU/CSU in several surveys for the first time. CDU fell from 33% to ~24% as AfD rose to ~27% in a GMS poll (January 2026).
2026 State Elections — Ongoing
~39–40% (Saxony-Anhalt polls)
Five state elections in 2026. AfD polling near 40% in Saxony-Anhalt and Mecklenburg-Vorpommern — figures that could force mainstream parties to consider cooperation. AfD won 19.5% in Rhineland-Palatinate (March 2026), more than doubling its 2021 result.

Key Structural Barrier
All major parties maintain a Brandmauer ("firewall") refusing coalition with the AfD. In eastern states polling near 40%, this forces mathematically complex 3–4 party coalitions that exclude the largest party. Whether this firewall holds through the 2026 eastern state elections is the central political question in Germany today.
