06 · Why People Vote AfD

KEY VOTER
MOTIVATIONS

🚫
Immigration
Anti-immigration remains the AfD's signature issue. The party advocates strict border controls and deportations. 41% of young Germans (14–29) cited immigration as a top concern in 2024 studies. The 2015 refugee crisis was the original catalyst.
#1 Issue
💸
Economic Anxiety
Cost-of-living pressures, energy costs, and deindustrialization hit eastern Germany hardest. AfD surged amid the collapse of the SPD-led "traffic light" coalition and growing discontent with mainstream economic management.
#2 Issue
🗳️
Protest / Veto Vote
Many AfD voters are driven by disillusionment rather than ideology. Over 100,000 voters switched from Die Linke to AfD in 2025 — a "veto vote" against the establishment. The party has never been in federal government, making it a credible anti-system choice.
~30–40%
🏛️
EU Scepticism
AfD advocates reducing EU competences and returning power to nation-states. Alice Weidel has called for dismantling the EU "superstructure." The party works with Viktor Orbán's Hungary as a model and sits in the Europe of Sovereign Nations group in the EP.
Core Policy
📱
Social Media Reach
Alice Weidel's TikTok and YouTube presence attracted young voters. MP Maximilian Krah targeted young men on social media explicitly. The AfD's new "Generation Deutschland" youth wing was launched in late 2025 to institutionalize this digital outreach.
Youth Driver
🧭
Eastern Identity
In eastern Germany, where AfD leads by large margins, a distinct post-reunification identity plays a role. Eastern voters perceive themselves as "left behind" economically and culturally. The party frames itself as the authentic voice of eastern Germany.
East Factor
07 · Timeline

THE AfD
TRAJECTORY

2013 — Founded
4.7%
Founded by economists opposing euro bailouts. Narrowly missed 5% threshold in first federal election. Focused on euro scepticism rather than immigration.
2017 — Enters Bundestag
12.6%
Capitalizing on 2015 refugee crisis, AfD entered Bundestag as third-largest party with 94 seats. Became the largest opposition party. Leadership pivoted decisively toward immigration and national identity.
2021 — Slips to 5th Place
10.4%
Lost 2.3 percentage points in 2021. Fell to fifth place. SPD won under Scholz; Greens surged. AfD's base remained in East but the party appeared to plateau at the national level.
2024 — State Election Breakthroughs
32.8% (Thuringia)
AfD became the first far-right party to win a state election plurality since the Nazi era — taking first in Thuringia. Also won first in Saxony and Brandenburg. Classified as "definitely extremist" in three eastern states by domestic intelligence.
Feb 2025 — Federal Election Record
20.8%
AfD doubled its federal vote share to 20.8% — its best-ever national result. Became 2nd-largest party with 151 seats. Won almost all direct constituencies in eastern Germany. CDU still refuses coalition with AfD.
May 2025 — Designated Extremist
Federal intelligence agency BfV classified the whole AfD (not just eastern branches) as a "confirmed right-wing extremist" organization in a 1,100-page report, finding it to be a "racist and anti-Muslim organisation." AfD called the move politically motivated.
Late 2025 — Polls Surge Past CDU
~26–27%
In December 2025–January 2026 polls, AfD recorded its highest-ever national figures — surpassing CDU/CSU in several surveys for the first time. CDU fell from 33% to ~24% as AfD rose to ~27%. GMS poll placed AfD at 27% vs CDU at 24%.
2026 State Elections — Upcoming
~39–40% (Saxony-Anhalt polls)
Five state elections in 2026. AfD is polling near 40% in Saxony-Anhalt and Mecklenburg-Vorpommern — figures that could force mainstream parties to consider cooperation. Baden-Württemberg (March 8), NRW (March 22), then eastern states in September.

Key Structural Barrier
All major parties maintain a Brandmauer ("firewall") refusing coalition with the AfD. In eastern states polling near 40%, this forces mathematically complex 3–4 party coalitions excluding the largest party. Whether this holds in 2026 is the central political question in Germany.
